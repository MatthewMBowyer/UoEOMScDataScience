Introduction

This e-portfolio captures my full learning experience throughout my latest module. Over twelve units and two major projects, I developed a stronger understanding of how research is planned, executed, and most importantly, communicated in a professional and comprehensible manner. The e-portfolio also pushes me to reflect critically on my own professional development. This is best achieved by following clear guidelines that promote critical reflection. (Satienchayakorn & Sanpatchayapong, 2025)

The module was a shift from the process of EDA, graphs, and reporting by adding initial steps of research and planning. This promoted further understanding in the project domain and knowledge of the latest research being done on the domain before I even began the EDA. This was emphasized to me as I completed the two major projects—one in a domain I am already familiar with and another in a domain I have yet to explore.

Reflection Approach: Gibbs’ Reflective Cycle

Throughout this e-portfolio, and given its success in previous submissions, I have used Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (The University of Edinburgh, 2024) as the foundation for reflection. With six stages described below, Gibbs model allows me to reflect deeply and systematically on each unit. This approach keeps me accountable for the work completed in each unit and provides an opportunity to elaborate further on those where more extensive work was undertaken.

  • Description: on the learning experience throughout each unit in the module.
  • Feelings: on my emotional response to initially viewing the units and then feedback on the complete projects.
  • Evaluation: of the entire unit, the good and the bad.
  • Analysis: on the units in hindsight.
  • Conclusion: on the unit and what I have learned and what I would have done differently.
  • Action Plan: on how I would deal with the next unit or module.
    •  

    For the final reflection, I also draw on Rolfe et al.’s (2001) “What? So what? Now what?” framework, which simplifies complex reflection into actionable goals. This dual-model approach is structured but flexible, and it encourages me to connect theory with practice. As Walland and Shaw (2020) argue, e-portfolios are both a process and a product: they’re about how we learn, not just what we produce. Majola (2025) emphasizes similar ideas, using terminology I resonate with — it’s not just a final product but a process of growth. This reflective structure captures that philosophy — showing my messy thinking, emotional responses, growth, and professional application.

    Reflection on Each Unit

    Unit 1 – Introduction to Research Methods. The Scientific Investigation and Ethics in Computing:

  • Description: This unit introduced the elements of the scientific method and the importance of ethics in research. I explored inductive and deductive reasoning and discussed ethical issues in computing, particularly around dark UX patterns.
  • Feelings: I was surprised to score 50 % on the reasoning quiz. A reminder that deduction and induction can be tricky to distinguish. Still, I found the topic fascinating, especially how ethical boundaries shape professional practice.
  • Evaluation: The readings helped me understand the foundation of scientific inquiry, but my initial confusion showed I needed to deepen my understanding of logic. The dark-patterns case study also helped me connect theory to real-world ethics.
  • Analysis: The quiz highlighted a clear knowledge gap. Ethics, while often overlooked, forms the backbone of professional credibility — I realised I must not treat it as an afterthought.
  • Conclusion: Ethics and logical reasoning are the twin pillars of sound research. I now see how they ensure transparency and reliability in computing.
  • Action Plan: Revisit inductive/deductive reasoning examples weekly until confident; retake the quiz and apply these reasoning types to my Cartrack work projects to reinforce understanding.
  • See my collaborative discussion on Ethics
    •    

    Unit 2 –Research Questions, the Literature Review and the Research Proposal

  • Description: I learned how to formulate clear, focused, and researchable questions, transforming general ideas into structured proposals.
  • Feelings: At first, developing precise questions felt overwhelming, but the framework provided helped simplify the process.
  • Evaluation: Early attempts were rough, yet by iterating and reviewing examples, I gained confidence in creating strong, relevant research questions.
  • Analysis: Good research begins with good questions, they guide everything from data collection to analysis. This shaped the foundation for my literature review on churn prediction.
  • Conclusion: Strong, well-crafted questions anchor all research efforts, ensuring purpose and direction.
  • Action Plan: Practise reframing broad work problems into research-style questions monthly to sharpen precision before my dissertation.
  • See my literature review plan - Early doors for the unit 7 project
    •  

    Unit 3 – Methodology and Research Methods

  • Description: This unit deepened my understanding of exploratory versus descriptive research and introduced qualitative, quantitative, and mixed methods.
  • Feelings: The theory felt dense at first, but I appreciated how each approach complements different research objectives.
  • Evaluation: I recognised the strengths of each method. Quantitative for measurement, qualitative for insight, and how both can merge into richer mixed methods.
  • Analysis: The choice of method must always align with the research question and the type of data sought. This became vital for my churn study, where numerical and behavioural data intersect.
  • Conclusion: Understanding research design clarified how methodology transforms abstract questions into executable studies.
  • Action Plan: Before each new Cartrack analysis, explicitly document whether my approach is exploratory or descriptive and justify my chosen method.
  • See my attempt at researching methods, data collection and skills required for the unit 7 project
    •  

    Unit 4 – Case Studies, Focus Groups and Observations

  • Description: I explored practical qualitative data-collection tools like case studies, focus groups, and observation, while submitting my literature-review outline for feedback.
  • Feelings: Submitting my outline was nerve-wracking but reassuring; feedback gave me clear direction.
  • Evaluation: This stage showed me how different methods produce distinct data types, each revealing unique perspectives.
  • Analysis: Balancing depth (qualitative) and scale (quantitative) is key. The feedback process also reinforced that refinement through critique strengthens research quality.
  • Conclusion: Iterative feedback is essential in refining both focus and scope in research.
  • Action Plan: Apply peer and tutor feedback loops early in my dissertation process rather than waiting until submission.
  • See the outline for my unit 7 project as well as feedback given

    Conclusion

    I started the module feeling like “This is my domain, machine learning is my day job.” Halfway in I realized I have not been emphasising resilience and ethics. By Unit 11, before presenting to execs, I felt the weight of translation: code alone won’t move the needle; stories do.

    Am I satisfied? Mostly. My technical bar rose; my communication bar nudged upward, my ethics bar has begun to grow. But i still have a long way to go. Reflections are only as good as action. I have added all actions to my to do list and I will complete them.
